Fractal Geometry in Ancient Architecture: The Case of Pompeii
================================================================

Fractals -- geometric objects with self-similar structure at
every scale -- were formally defined by Benoit Mandelbrot in
1975. Yet evidence of fractal thinking appears in human
artifacts stretching back thousands of years. The Roman city
of Pompeii, preserved under volcanic ash since 79 AD, offers
a remarkable case study in unconscious fractal design.

---

What Are Fractals?
---

A fractal is a set for which the Hausdorff-Besicovitch
dimension strictly exceeds the topological dimension. In
simpler terms, a fractal displays detail at every scale.
The Mandelbrot set, the Sierpinski triangle, and the Koch
snowflake are canonical examples.

The fractal dimension D is related to the scaling ratio
by:

  D = log(N) / log(1/r)

where N is the number of self-similar pieces and r is the
scaling factor. For the Sierpinski triangle, N = 3 and
r = 1/2, giving D = log(3)/log(2) = 1.585.

---

Self-Similarity in Roman Mosaics
---

Pompeian floor mosaics exhibit striking self-similarity.
The geometric borders typically feature nested patterns:
a large-scale meander (Greek key) containing smaller
meanders, which themselves contain still smaller
iterations. The pattern repeats at three or four scales
before reaching the resolution limit of the tesserae.

Mathematical analysis of these borders reveals
box-counting dimensions of approximately 1.3 to 1.5,
indicating fractal-like structure. The craftsmen who
created these mosaics were not aware of fractal
geometry, but they intuitively grasped the aesthetic
power of repeating patterns across scales.

The famous Alexander Mosaic from the House of the
Faun depicts a battle scene with crowd dynamics that
exhibit fractal clustering -- groups within groups,
with size distributions following power laws.

---

Scaling Laws in Urban Planning
---

Pompeii's urban layout demonstrates scaling relationships
consistent with fractal principles. The street network
has a hierarchical structure: main thoroughfares (cardo
and decumanus) branch into secondary streets, which
branch into alleys and passages. The width distribution
follows a power law:

  N(w > W) ~ W^(-alpha)

where alpha is approximately 1.2, consistent with
fractal urban models developed by Michael Batty and
Paul Longley in the 1990s.

Building sizes within the city blocks also follow
power-law distributions. The few largest buildings
(domus, public structures) dominate the total area,
while numerous small shops and workshops fill the
intervening space. This distribution is characteristic
of fractal systems and maximizes space utilization.

---

Wall Paintings and Fractal-Like Patterns
---

Pompeian wall paintings of the Four Styles contain
fractal-like decorative elements. Style II paintings
feature architectural trompe l'oeil with columns within
columns, arches within arches, creating visual depth
through self-similar layering.

The Fourth Style wall paintings from the Villa of the
Mysteries demonstrate a hierarchical visual structure:
large figurative scenes contain smaller decorative
borders, which themselves contain micro-patterns of
vegetal scrollwork. Box-counting analysis of high-
resolution photographs yields dimensions between 1.2
and 1.6, indicating genuine multi-scale complexity.

---

Connection to Modern Fractal Architecture
---

Modern architects explicitly use fractal principles
in design. Architect Nikos Salingaros argues that
buildings with fractal detail at multiple scales are
perceived as more beautiful and comfortable than those
without. His fractal criteria for architecture include:

  1. At least four distinct scales of detail
  2. Each scale contains sub-elements of the next
  3. Elements at each scale are clearly distinct
  4. Transitions between scales are smooth

Pompeian buildings satisfy these criteria unconsciously.
The facades along the Via dell'Abbondanza show large-
scale symmetry, medium-scale rhythm of windows and
doors, fine-scale decoration of capitals and moldings,
and micro-scale texture of stone and stucco.

---

Implications for Polynomial Art
---

Fractal geometry in ancient art suggests that human
aesthetic preferences are tuned to multi-scale
mathematical structure. Polynomial art can incorporate
fractal principles through recursive subdivision,
multi-scale parameter variation, and hierarchical
composition. By understanding what made Pompeian mosaics
beautiful to Roman eyes, we gain insight into the
mathematical foundations of visual appeal that persist
across millennia.
