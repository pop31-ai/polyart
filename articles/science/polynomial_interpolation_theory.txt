Polynomial Interpolation: From Lagrange to Least Squares
================================================================

Polynomial interpolation is one of the oldest and most
fundamental problems in numerical analysis. Given a set of
data points, find a polynomial that passes through all of
them. The solution underpins everything from computer graphics
to scientific computing.

---

The Lagrange Form
---

Joseph-Louis Lagrange (1736-1813) gave the first elegant
solution. For n+1 points (x_0, y_0), ..., (x_n, y_n), the
interpolating polynomial is:

  P(x) = sum_{k=0}^{n} y_k * L_k(x)

where the Lagrange basis polynomials are:

  L_k(x) = prod_{j=0, j!=k}^{n} (x - x_j) / (x_k - x_j)

Each L_k(x) equals 1 at x = x_k and 0 at all other nodes.
The formula is theoretically beautiful but computationally
inefficient: O(n^2) operations to evaluate, and adding a
new point requires recomputing everything.

---

Newton's Divided Differences
---

Isaac Newton (1643-1727) developed a more practical approach.
Define divided differences recursively:

  f[x_i] = y_i
  f[x_i, ..., x_{i+k}] = (f[x_{i+1},...,x_{i+k}] -
                           f[x_i,...,x_{i+k-1}]) /
                          (x_{i+k} - x_i)

The interpolating polynomial becomes:

  P(x) = f[x_0] + f[x_0,x_1](x-x_0) +
         f[x_0,x_1,x_2](x-x_0)(x-x_1) + ...

This form is computationally superior: adding a new data
point requires only one additional term, not a full
recomputation. The Newton form is also easier to analyze
for error bounds.

---

Chebyshev Nodes and Minimax Approximation
---

Pafnuty Chebyshev (1821-1894) discovered that the placement
of interpolation nodes dramatically affects accuracy. For
equidistant nodes, interpolation error grows exponentially
as the degree increases -- a problem later identified by
Carl Runge (1901).

Chebyshev nodes for the interval [-1, 1] are:

  x_k = cos((2k+1)*pi / (2(n+1))), k = 0, 1, ..., n

These nodes cluster near the endpoints, where polynomial
oscillation is worst. Interpolation at Chebyshev nodes
achieves near-minimax accuracy: the maximum error is
close to the theoretical best polynomial approximation.

---

Runge's Phenomenon
---

Runge showed that for the function f(x) = 1/(1 + 25x^2),
interpolation at equidistant points diverges as the degree
increases. The interpolated polynomial oscillates wildly
near the endpoints, even though the function is smooth.

This counterintuitive result has profound implications:
higher-degree polynomials are not always better
approximators. The lesson for polynomial art is clear:
choosing the right degree matters more than maximizing it.

---

Least Squares Fitting
---

When data points exceed the polynomial degree, exact
interpolation becomes impossible. The least squares method
finds the polynomial that minimizes the total squared error:

  min_{c_0,...,c_n} sum_{i=1}^{m} (y_i - P(x_i))^2

This leads to the normal equations:

  (X^T X) c = X^T y

where X is the Vandermonde matrix with entries X_{ij} = x_i^j.

Least squares is the method behind numpy polyfit and is
the mathematical engine of polynomial art generation.

---

Error Analysis and Degree Selection
---

The interpolation error for polynomial P_n of degree n is:

  f(x) - P_n(x) = f^{(n+1)}(xi) / (n+1)! *
                   prod_{k=0}^{n} (x - x_k)

for some xi in the interval. This reveals two competing
effects: the derivative factor grows with function
complexity, while the factorial denominator grows with
degree. Optimal degree balances these forces.

In practice, the leave-one-out cross-validation error
or the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) guides degree
selection for polynomial art, ensuring curves are smooth
yet faithful to the underlying data.

---

Application in Polynomial Art
---

Polynomial art leverages interpolation theory to transform
raw data into visual beauty. By carefully selecting
polation nodes, choosing degree via error analysis, and
using least squares for noisy data, artists and
programmers create curves that are both mathematically
sound and aesthetically compelling.
