The Mathematics of Emotion: Polynomial Expression Rendering
============================================================

Abstract
--------
Facial expressions encode emotional states through the coordinated
movement of facial muscles, producing characteristic geometric
deformations of the face surface. This article presents a polynomial
framework for modeling these deformations, implementing the Facial
Action Coding System (FACS) as a set of polynomial basis functions.
We derive polynomial representations for brow curvature, eye aperture,
mouth shape, and their combinations for 10 basic emotions, enabling
procedural generation of emotionally expressive faces.

1. Introduction
---------------
Paul Ekman's Facial Action Coding System (FACS) decomposes facial
expressions into individual Action Units (AUs), each corresponding
to the contraction of specific facial muscles. While FACS provides
a descriptive framework, a computational system requires quantitative
models of facial surface deformation. Polynomial functions offer
an ideal representation: they are smooth, efficiently evaluable,
and their coefficients map directly to facial muscle activation
parameters. The polyart.emotion module implements this mapping.

2. Facial Action Coding System Overview
-----------------------------------------
FACS defines 46 primary Action Units, plus supplementary AUs. The
most relevant for emotion rendering:

  AU1:  Inner Brow Raise       (frontalis, pars medialis)
  AU2:  Outer Brow Raise       (frontalis, pars lateralis)
  AU4:  Brow Lowerer           (corrugator supercilii)
  AU5:  Upper Lid Raise        (levator palpebrae superioris)
  AU6:  Cheek Raise            (orbicularis oculi, orbita part)
  AU7:  Lid Tightener          (orbicularis oculi, palpebral part)
  AU9:  Nose Wrinkler          (nasalis)
  AU10: Upper Lip Raiser       (levator labii superioris)
  AU12: Lip Corner Puller      (zygomaticus major)
  AU15: Lip Corner Depressor   (depressor anguli oris)
  AU17: Chin Raiser            (mentalis)
  AU20: Lip Stretcher          (risorius)
  AU25: Lips Part              (depressor labii inferioris)
  AU26: Jaw Drop               (masseter, temporalis)

Each AU produces a characteristic surface deformation that can be
approximated by polynomial displacement fields.

3. Polynomial Brow Curves
--------------------------
The eyebrow curve in the frontal plane is parameterized by arc
length s:

  y_brow(s) = y_0(s) + delta_AU1 * f_1(s) + delta_AU2 * f_2(s)
                + delta_AU4 * f_4(s)

where y_0(s) is the neutral brow shape and f_i(s) are AU-specific
deformation functions:

  f_1(s) = A_1 * exp(-(s-s_peak1)^2 / (2*sigma_1^2))
  f_2(s) = A_2 * (s/L)^alpha * exp(-(s-s_peak2)^2 / (2*sigma_2^2))
  f_4(s) = -A_4 * (1 - s/L)^beta

The inner brow raise (AU1) peaks near the nose bridge. The outer
brow raise (AU2) peaks at the lateral third. The brow lowerer (AU4)
depresses the medial brow with a medial-lateral gradient.

Polynomial approximation using Chebyshev basis:

  y_brow(s) = sum_{k=0}^{6} c_k * T_k(2*s/L - 1)

where T_k are Chebyshev polynomials. The degree-6 expansion captures
brow curvature with sub-millimeter accuracy.

4. Eye Aperture Functions
--------------------------
Eye openness is controlled by the vertical distance between upper
and lower eyelid margins at the pupil centerline:

  h_eye(t) = h_0 + delta_AU5 * g_5(t) - delta_AU7 * g_7(t)

For blink dynamics, the aperture follows a polynomial approximation
of the measured velocity profile:

  h_blink(t) = h_max * (1 - (t/T_blink)^2 * (3 - 2*t/T_blink))

This is a smoothstep function (Hermite polynomial) where T_blink
is the blink duration (typically 300-400ms). The natural blink
cycle consists of rapid close (150ms) and slower open (250ms).

Eye shape in the frontal plane is an almond-like curve:

  x(t) = a * cos(t)
  y(t) = b * sin(t) * (1 + epsilon * cos(2*t))

where epsilon controls the almond sharpness (epsilon=0 gives
ellipse, epsilon>0 gives pointed corners).

5. Mouth Curvature Model
--------------------------
The mouth is the most geometrically complex facial feature.
The lip line (where lips meet) is a polynomial curve:

  y_mouth(x) = sum_{i=0}^{5} m_i * x^i

The neutral mouth has a slight downward curvature at the corners.
Emotional states modify this curve:

  AU12 (smile):  y_mouth(x) += S * (x^2 - x_max^2) / x_max^2
  AU15 (frown):  y_mouth(x) -= F * (x^2 - x_max^2) / x_max^2

The lip fullness is modeled by a separate polynomial for upper
and lower lip profiles:

  y_upper(x) = sum_{i=0}^{4} u_i * T_i(x/a)
  y_lower(x) = sum_{i=0}^{4} l_i * T_i(x/a)

Mouth opening (AU25, AU26) adds a vertical displacement between
upper and lower lip curves:

  y_lower_opened(x) = y_lower(x) - d_open * (1 - (x/a)^2)^p

6. Emotion Blending
---------------------
Basic emotions combine multiple AUs with characteristic intensities.
We define an emotion vector E = (e_1, e_2, ..., e_46) where e_i
is the intensity of AU_i (0.0 to 1.0). Basic emotions as vectors:

  Joy:        E_joy = [AU6:0.8, AU12:1.0, AU25:0.3]
  Sadness:    E_sad = [AU1:0.7, AU4:0.5, AU15:0.6, AU17:0.3]
  Anger:      E_ang = [AU4:0.9, AU5:0.4, AU7:0.8, AU23:0.6]
  Fear:       E_fear = [AU1:0.8, AU2:0.7, AU4:0.3, AU5:0.9, AU20:0.6]
  Disgust:    E_dis = [AU9:1.0, AU10:0.7, AU15:0.4, AU17:0.3]
  Surprise:   E_sur = [AU1:0.9, AU2:0.8, AU5:1.0, AU25:0.5, AU26:0.7]
  Contempt:   E_con = [AU12:0.3]  (unilateral)
  Trust:      E_tru = [AU6:0.4, AU12:0.5]
  Anticipation: E_ant = [AU12:0.6, AU25:0.2]
  Sadness_grief: E_sg = [AU1:1.0, AU4:0.6, AU15:0.8, AU17:0.5, AU25:0.3]

Blended emotions use weighted averaging:

  E_blend = alpha * E_a + (1-alpha) * E_b

where alpha in [0,1] controls the blend ratio. The resulting
facial geometry is the superposition of individual AU deformations.

7. State Transitions
---------------------
Emotional transitions are modeled as time-varying AU intensities:

  e_i(t) = e_i_base + (e_i_target - e_i_base) * phi(t; t_start, t_end)

where phi is a smooth transition function:

  phi(t; t_s, t_e) = 0              if t < t_s
                    = (t-t_s)^2 *    if t_s <= t < (t_s+t_e)/2
                      (3(t_s+t_e) - 2t) / (t_e-t_s)^3
                    = 1              if t >= t_e

This cubic Hermite spline ensures C1 continuity at transition
boundaries. Transition durations vary by AU:

  - Mouth AUs: 200-500ms (fast response)
  - Brow AUs: 300-700ms (moderate)
  - Eye AUs: 100-400ms (blink-speed for AU5/7)

8. Complete Emotion Rendering Pipeline
----------------------------------------
The polyart.emotion pipeline:

  1. Define emotion vector E (or load from preset)
  2. Map E to AU intensities e_i
  3. For each facial region (brow, eye, nose, mouth):
     a. Compute polynomial displacement from neutral
     b. Apply AU-specific deformation functions
     c. Sum contributions weighted by intensity
  4. Combine regional displacements into face mesh
  5. Apply temporal smoothing for animation
  6. Render with polyart.renderer

Usage:

  from polyart.emotion import EmotionEngine, EmotionPreset

  engine = EmotionEngine(face_model='neutral.obj')
  engine.set_emotion(EmotionPreset.JOY, intensity=0.8)
  engine.render('joyful_face.png')
  
  # Transition from joy to sadness
  engine.transition(EmotionPreset.SADNESS, duration_ms=1000)
  engine.animate('transition.mp4', fps=30)

9. Polynomial Expression Energy
---------------------------------
The expression energy metric quantifies emotional intensity:

  E_total = sum_{i=1}^{46} w_i * e_i^2

where w_i are AU importance weights. This metric correlates with
human ratings of emotional intensity. The energy ranges from 0
(neutral face) to approximately 10 (maximal expression).

10. Conclusion
---------------
Polynomial representations provide a mathematically rigorous and
computationally efficient framework for facial expression modeling.
By mapping FACS Action Units to polynomial basis functions, we
enable procedural generation of emotionally expressive faces.
The blending and transition mechanisms allow smooth animation
between emotional states, supporting applications in animation,
affective computing, and psychological research.
