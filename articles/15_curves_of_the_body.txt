Polynomial Curves of the Human Body: A Complete Atlas
======================================================

Abstract
--------
The human body exhibits rich geometric structure amenable to polynomial
representation. This article presents a systematic atlas of polynomial
curves describing skeletal architecture, muscle contours, skin
silhouettes, vascular networks, neural pathways, limb geometry, and
cranial topography. Each curve class is derived from anatomical
measurements and expressed as parametric or implicit polynomials
suitable for computational modeling within the polyart.anatomy framework.

1. Introduction
---------------
Anatomical illustration has a long history of using mathematical
curves to represent body forms. From Vesalius's woodcuts to modern
CT reconstruction, the representation of biological geometry
requires careful selection of basis functions. Polynomial curves
offer a natural choice: they are universal approximators, efficiently
computable, and their coefficients carry intuitive geometric meaning.
The polyart.anatomy module provides a comprehensive library of such
representations.

2. Skeletal Curves
-------------------
2.1 Spinal Curvature
The spine follows a characteristic S-curve described by three
polynomial segments:

  Cervical: y = a_1*x + b_1*x^2 + c_1*x^3    (x in [0, 0.12H])
  Thoracic: y = a_2*x + b_2*x^2 + c_2*x^3    (x in [0.12H, 0.52H])
  Lumbar:   y = a_3*x + b_3*x^2 + c_3*x^3    (x in [0.52H, 0.68H])

where H is total body height. The sagittal plane curvature values
for an average adult male:

  Cervical lordosis:  curvature = 0.034 /cm
  Thoracic kyphosis:  curvature = 0.028 /cm
  Lumbar lordosis:    curvature = 0.041 /cm

The polyart.anatomy.spinal_curve() function constructs a piecewise
cubic spline matching these curvature values with C1 continuity.

2.2 Rib Geometry
Each rib follows a curved path from the spine to the sternum.
A single rib can be modeled as an elliptical arc in 3D:

  x(t) = R*cos(t) + dx
  y(t) = r*sin(t) + dy
  z(t) = z0 + slope*t

where R is the major radius (rib length), r is the minor radius
(thoracic depth), and slope captures the downward tilt. For ribs
1-7 (true ribs), slope is small; for ribs 8-12 (false/floating
ribs), slope increases progressively.

2.3 Pelvic Architecture
The pelvic ring is approximated by a superellipse:

  |x/a|^n + |y/b|^n = 1

with a = 14.5cm, b = 10.2cm, n = 2.4 for adult males. The
sacral curve follows a fifth-degree polynomial fit to CT data:

  y = sum_{i=0}^{5} c_i * x^i

3. Muscle Contours
-------------------
3.1 Biceps Brachii
The biceps contour in longitudinal section is a Gaussian-modulated
sine wave:

  r(s) = r_rest + A * exp(-(s-s_peak)^2 / (2*sigma^2)) * sin(pi*s/L)

where s is the position along the humerus, L is the muscle length,
A is the peak belly radius, and sigma controls the taper.

3.2 Quadriceps Group
The quadriceps cross-section at any point along the femur is
approximated by a superellipse with 4-fold near-symmetry. The
polyart.anatomy.muscle_contour() function generates these contours
for any muscle group given attachment points and belly parameters.

3.3 Abdominal Wall
The rectus abdominis is modeled as parallel curves:

  x(t) = x_center + w(t) * cos(theta)
  y(t) = y_center + w(t) * sin(theta)

where w(t) varies with the tendinous intersection pattern, typically
3-4 horizontal inscriptions creating the characteristic segmented
appearance.

4. Skin Silhouettes
--------------------
4.1 Standing Profile
The full-body standing profile (sagittal plane) is captured by a
single polynomial of degree 8 or by piecewise cubics:

  x(y) = sum_{i=0}^{8} a_i * (y/H)^i

where y is height from the ground and H is total height. The
coefficients a_i encode the characteristic contours of forehead,
nose, chin, neck, chest, abdomen, buttock, calf, and heel.

4.2 Hand Outline
The hand silhouette uses a polar representation with 5 lobe
contributions (one per finger) and a palm base:

  r(theta) = r_palm + sum_{k=1}^{5} A_k * exp(-((theta-theta_k)/sigma_k)^2)

Each finger lobe has its own amplitude A_k and angular position
theta_k, varying with hand size and gesture.

5. Vascular Networks
---------------------
5.1 Arterial Branching
Arterial trees follow Murray's law for optimal flow:

  r_parent^3 = sum_{i=1}^{n} r_daughter_i^3

Each arterial segment is a tapered cylinder described by:

  r(s) = r_0 * (1 - s/L * (1 - (r_L/r_0)))^p

where r_0 is the proximal radius, r_L is the distal radius, L is
segment length, and p is typically 1.0 for linear taper.

5.2 Venous Returns
Veins exhibit more tortuous paths than arteries. Their curvature
is modeled as:

  kappa(s) = kappa_0 + A * sin(2*pi*s/lambda + phi)

where the waviness parameters (A, lambda) increase toward distal
segments.

6. Neural Pathways
-------------------
6.1 Spinal Nerve Exits
Nerves exit the spinal column at regular angular intervals. In the
thoracic region:

  theta_n = theta_0 + n * 180/N

where N is the total nerve pair count at that level and theta_0 is
the initial offset.

6.2 Peripheral Nerve Curves
Major peripheral nerves follow paths that minimize total length
while avoiding joints. These paths approximate geodesic curves on
a deformed cylinder (the limb surface).

7. Limb Geometry
----------------
7.1 Upper Limb Proportions
Da Vinci's proportional rules for the arm:

  - Elbow to wrist = 0.25 * arm_span
  - Hand length = 0.11 * arm_span
  - Finger length follows geometric series per digit

The polynomial representation of forearm taper:

  r(z) = r_elbow * (1 - 0.35*(z/L)^1.5)

7.2 Lower Limb Proportions
The femur-tibia ratio is approximately 1.08 in adult humans.
The leg contour combines two polynomials for anterior and posterior:

  Anterior: x_f(t) = a_f*t + b_f*t^2
  Posterior: x_p(t) = a_p*t + b_p*t^2 + c_p*t^3

8. Cranial Topography
----------------------
8.1 Skull Vault
The cranial vault is an ellipsoidal shell:

  (x/a)^2 + (y/b)^2 + (z/c)^2 = 1

with a = 8.2cm, b = 7.8cm, c = 7.5cm (adult averages). The
sagittal profile is a degree-6 polynomial fit:

  z(x) = c_0 + c_2*x^2 + c_4*x^4 + c_6*x^6

(symmetry eliminates odd terms).

8.2 Facial Profile
The facial profile (nasion to chin) follows a polynomial curve:

  x(z) = sum_{i=0}^{5} f_i * z^i

with coefficients encoding forehead slope, nasal projection, lip
prominence, and chin position. The anthropometric measurements
from Farkas (1994) provide the reference values.

9. Implementation in polyart.anatomy
--------------------------------------
The polyart.anatomy module provides:

  - AnatomicalModel class with full body representation
  - Joint hierarchy for skeletal articulation
  - MuscleAttachment for deformable muscle simulation
  - SkinMesh for surface generation from skeletal + muscle data
  - VascularTree and NerveNetwork for subsystem modeling

Usage example:

  from polyart.anatomy import AnatomicalModel

  model = AnatomicalModel(sex='male', age=30, height_cm=175)
  model.set_pose('standing', arms='sides')
  spine = model.get_spine_curve()
  muscles = model.get_muscle_contours()
  skin = model.get_silhouette('sagittal')
  model.export_stl('anatomy_mesh.stl')

10. Conclusion
--------------
The human body's geometric complexity is amenable to systematic
polynomial representation. By organizing curves into anatomical
layers (skeletal, muscular, integumentary, vascular, neural), we
create a modular atlas that serves both artistic illustration and
medical visualization. The polyart.anatomy framework provides the
computational infrastructure for this atlas, with extensibility
for research-specific measurements and pathologies.
