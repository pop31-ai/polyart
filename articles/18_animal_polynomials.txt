Animal Forms Through Polynomial Lenses
=======================================

Abstract
--------
Animal morphology encompasses a vast range of forms shaped by evolution.
This article presents polynomial representations for diverse animal
forms: the lion's radial mane, eagle wing airfoil curves, wolf
snout geometry, horse body proportions, snake spline locomotion,
and the composite mythological dragon. Each model captures essential
geometric features through polynomial approximation, enabling both
scientific visualization and artistic generation within the
polyart.fauna framework.

1. Introduction
---------------
The animal kingdom presents an extraordinary diversity of form, from
the sinuous curves of serpents to the powerful radial symmetry of a
lion's mane. Representing these forms mathematically requires careful
selection of coordinate systems and basis functions. Polynomial
curves provide a natural choice due to their flexibility, compactness,
and the physical interpretability of their coefficients. The
polyart.fauna module provides a library of animal form generators
built on these polynomial foundations.

2. Lion Mane as Radial Polynomial
-----------------------------------
The lion's mane is one of nature's most dramatic radial forms.
Viewed from above, the mane radiates symmetrically from the face
center. The radial extent varies with angle:

  R(theta) = R_0 + sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k * cos(k*theta + phi_k)

For a symmetric mane (n=3 captures the primary asymmetry):

  R(theta) = R_0 + a_1*cos(theta + phi_1) + a_2*cos(2*theta + phi_2)
               + a_3*cos(3*theta + phi_3)

The mane has two distinct regions:

  - Short face-framing hair: R_face ~ R_0 * (1 + 0.1*cos(3*theta))
  - Long outer mane: R_outer ~ R_0 * (1 + 0.4*cos(theta) + 0.2*cos(2*theta))

The transition between regions uses a radial smoothstep:

  R_total(r, theta) = R_face(theta) * (1-S(r)) + R_outer(theta) * S(r)

where S(r) = 3*(r/R_0)^2 - 2*(r/R_0)^3 is the Hermite interpolation.

Individual hair strands within the mane follow polynomial paths:

  strand(t) = P_start + t*(P_end - P_start) + curl * t^2 * direction

The curl parameter creates the characteristic outward-arching
profile of mane hairs.

3. Eagle Wing Airfoil Curves
-----------------------------
The eagle wing cross-section (airfoil) follows the NACA polynomial
series. A NACA 4-digit airfoil is defined by:

  y(x) = t/0.2 * (2.969*sqrt(x) - 1.260*x - 3.516*x^2
                   + 2.843*x^3 - 1.015*x^4)

where:
  x = chordwise position (0 to 1)
  t = maximum thickness as fraction of chord
  y = half-thickness at position x

For an eagle wing, the airfoil varies along the span:
  - Root (near body): thick, high camber (t~0.15, camber~0.06)
  - Mid-span: moderate (t~0.12, camber~0.04)
  - Tip: thin, low camber (t~0.08, camber~0.02)

The camber line itself is a polynomial:

  y_c(x) = m/((1-p)^2) * ((1-2*p) + 2*p*x - x^2)

where m is the maximum camber and p is its position along chord.
The polyart.fauna.airfoil() function generates these curves:

  from polyart.fauna import airfoil

  wing_root = airfoil(naca='2412', span_position=0.0)
  wing_tip  = airfoil(naca='2108', span_position=1.0)

4. Wolf Snout Geometry
-----------------------
The wolf snout (muzzle) combines several polynomial surfaces.
In profile (sagittal plane):

  x(z) = sum_{i=0}^{4} s_i * z^i

where z is the vertical axis (dorsal-ventral). The dorsal line
(from forehead to nose tip) is nearly straight with a slight
convex curve:

  x_dorsal(z) = s_0 + s_2*z^2 + s_4*z^4

The ventral line (lower jaw profile) follows:

  x_ventral(z) = v_0 + v_1*z + v_2*z^2 + v_3*z^3

The cross-sectional shape (coronal plane) transitions from
circular at the eyes to triangular at the nose tip:

  y(z) = w(z) * cos(phi)
  x(z) = w(z) * sin(phi)

where w(z) controls the width and the shape parameter varies
from circular (n=2) to triangular (n=4) along the snout:

  |y/w|^n + |x/w|^n = 1

5. Horse Proportions (Da Vinci Rules)
--------------------------------------
Leonardo da Vinci codified equine proportions in his notebooks.
The canonical horse proportions:

  - Head length = 1/3 body length
  - Neck length = 1/2 body length
  - Leg length (ground to elbow) = body length
  - Body length = 3 * head length
  - Height at withers = 2 * head length

The polynomial representation of the horse body profile
(lateral view) uses a degree-6 polynomial:

  y_body(x) = sum_{i=0}^{6} h_i * (x/L)^i

where L is the body length and h_i encode:
  h_0: chest depth
  h_1: underline slope
  h_2-h_3: belly curvature
  h_4: croup rise
  h_5-h_6: tail-head transition

The legs are represented as articulated polynomial segments:

  upper_leg(t) = hip_point + t*(knee_point - hip_point) + lean*t^2
  lower_leg(t) = knee_point + t*(fetlock_point - knee_point)

The lean parameter captures the characteristic backward angle of
the upper leg and forward angle of the lower leg.

6. Snake Spline
----------------
Snake locomotion is represented as a traveling polynomial wave:

  x(s, t) = x_0(s) + A(s) * sin(k*s - omega*t + phi_0)
  y(s, t) = y_0(s) + B(s) * cos(k*s - omega*t + phi_0)

where:
  s = arc length parameter along the body
  t = time
  A(s), B(s) = amplitude envelopes (tapering at head and tail)
  k = wave number (2*pi/wavelength)
  omega = angular frequency

The snake body cross-section is elliptical:

  x(theta) = a * cos(theta)
  y(theta) = b * sin(theta)

with a > b (flattened ventrally). The ratio a/b varies by species:
  - Constrictors: a/b ~ 1.2 (nearly circular)
  - Vipers: a/b ~ 1.5 (more flattened)

Head geometry uses the wolf snout model scaled and elongated,
with additional triangular scale pattern polynomials.

7. Dragon as Composite Myth
-----------------------------
The dragon is a composite of real animal forms, requiring the
integration of multiple polynomial models:

  Body:   Equine base (polynomial from Section 5)
  Neck:   Serpentine curve (polynomial wave from Section 6)
  Head:   Canine snout (Section 4) with added horn polynomials
  Wings:  Chiropteran airfoil (bat-like, from Section 3 variant)
  Tail:   Elongated serpentine taper
  Scales: Overlapping radial pattern (R(theta) = R_base + cos(k*theta))

The dragon assembly in polyart:

  from polyart.fauna import DragonBuilder

  dragon = DragonBuilder(
      body_type='equine',
      neck_segments=12,
      wing_type='chiropteran',
      wing_span_ratio=1.5,
      tail_length=2.5,
      scale_pattern='imbricate'
  )
  dragon.set_pose('soaring')
  dragon.generate()
  dragon.render('dragon.png', style='wireframe')

The body-neck junction uses polynomial blending to ensure C2
continuity between the horse-like torso and the snake-like neck.

8. Comparative Morphometric Analysis
--------------------------------------
We can compare animal forms using polynomial signature vectors.
Each animal is represented by a vector of polynomial coefficients
from its primary body curves. The similarity between species i
and j is:

  sim(i,j) = 1 / (1 + ||c_i - c_j||_2 / ||c_i||_2)

where c_i, c_j are the normalized coefficient vectors. This metric
reveals evolutionary relationships consistent with phylogenetic
classification.

9. Conclusion
--------------
Polynomial representations capture the essential geometric features
of animal forms across the full range of biological diversity. From
the radial symmetry of a lion's mane to the traveling waves of
snake locomotion, polynomial curves and surfaces provide both
scientific accuracy and artistic flexibility. The polyart.fauna
module integrates these models into a coherent framework for
computational animal morphology.
