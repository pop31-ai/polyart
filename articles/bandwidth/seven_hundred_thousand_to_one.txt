700,000:1: The Number That Will Change the Internet

There are numbers that describe incremental progress. A ten percent improvement in efficiency. A twenty percent reduction in cost. A fifty percent increase in speed. These numbers appear in technical papers and marketing materials. They are notable but not transformative.

Then there are numbers that describe a fundamental shift in capability. The number 700,000 to 1 is one of these.

The number represents the compression ratio achieved by the Polynomial World Transmission Protocol for structured video scenes. A fifteen-minute video of a man walking through ancient Pompeii, recorded in 4K at sixty frames per second, requires approximately 400 gigabytes of data using traditional video encoding. The same content, transmitted as a PWTP polynomial description, requires approximately 600 kilobytes. The ratio of these two numbers is approximately 700,000 to 1.

This is not an incremental improvement. It is not a better video codec that achieves 50 percent better compression. It is a fundamentally different approach to the problem of transmitting visual information. Instead of encoding each frame as a grid of pixels, PWTP encodes the mathematical structure of the scene: the static world as polynomial surfaces, the dynamic elements as polynomial trajectories.

The number will appear in every networking textbook because it represents the largest single improvement in communication efficiency in the history of the field. The invention of digital communication itself, which replaced analog transmission, achieved improvements of perhaps 100 to 1 over the analog systems it replaced. PWTP achieves 700,000 to 1 over the digital systems it replaces.

The walk through Pompeii is the demonstration that proves the theorem. It is a simple, comprehensible scenario: a man walks through ancient ruins. Every person can visualize this scenario. Every person can understand what 400 gigabytes and 600 kilobytes mean in practical terms. The demonstration is accessible in a way that abstract technical specifications are not.

The implications of the number ripple outward from the demonstration. If video bandwidth can be reduced by 700,000 to 1, the entire internet infrastructure changes. Data centers shrink. Networks become uncongested. Mobile devices serve as full-fidelity video receivers on any connection. The digital divide narrows to the width of a polynomial.

The number 700,000 to 1 is not the end of the story. Quantum enhancement of polynomial encoding may push the ratio higher. New applications of polynomial representation may extend the concept beyond video to other forms of data. The fifteen-minute walk through Pompeii is the beginning.

But the number itself is the headline. 700,000 to 1. Remember it. It will change everything.
