If Every Video Was a Polynomial: The Future of Streaming

Netflix consumes approximately fifteen percent of global internet bandwidth. YouTube consumes another fifteen percent. Together, these two platforms alone account for nearly a third of all data transmitted worldwide. Add video calls, social media video, surveillance feeds, and every other form of moving image, and video accounts for eighty percent of internet traffic.

Now imagine a world where every video is a polynomial.

A Netflix film, instead of being streamed at five megabits per second, is transmitted as a polynomial description of its scenes. A fifteen-minute segment with a character walking through a city requires 600 kilobytes. At typical streaming bitrates, the same segment requires several gigabytes. The Netflix application would download the polynomial description, evaluate it locally, and render each frame on demand.

YouTube tutorials, currently streamed at two to five megabits per second, would require ten kilobits per second. A university lecture where a professor walks across a stage and writes on a board: the stage is a static polynomial surface, the professor's trajectory is a cubic polynomial, the board content is a static texture. Total transmission: perhaps 200 kilobytes for the entire hour-long lecture.

Video calls would transform entirely. Instead of transmitting thirty frames per second of compressed video, each participant transmits a polynomial description of their movement and the static background behind them. A video call at five kilobits per second instead of five megabits per second. A thousandfold reduction in bandwidth for the most bandwidth-intensive communication application in daily use.

The technology works because most video content has the same structure as the Pompeii walk. A static environment with smooth motion occurring within it. Characters walk. Cameras pan. Objects move along predictable paths. The mathematical structure of reality is polynomial, and polynomials are the optimal representation.

The implications extend beyond bandwidth savings. If video requires 700,000 times less data, the entire infrastructure of the internet changes. CDN costs collapse. Data center storage requirements shrink. Mobile networks can serve video to every device on Earth simultaneously. The digital divide, which is fundamentally a bandwidth divide, narrows to a gap that any 2G connection can bridge.

Streaming at polynomial speed means a student in rural Bangladesh watches the same educational content as a student in Manhattan, both at the same quality, both with no buffering. It means a surgeon in a remote clinic receives real-time 3D guidance from a specialist in Tokyo. It means every human with a device and a connection has access to the full visual library of human knowledge.

The fifteen-minute walk through Pompeii, at 600 kilobytes, is the proof. The future of streaming is not faster networks. It is smaller data.
