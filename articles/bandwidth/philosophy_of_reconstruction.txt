The Philosophy of Reconstruction: Is a Polynomial Walk the Same Walk?

A man walks through Pompeii. The walk is captured. The capture is converted to polynomial coefficients. The coefficients are transmitted. A receiver evaluates the polynomials and renders the walk. The viewer sees a man walking through Pompeii.

Is this the same walk?

The question is not about data formats or compression ratios. It is about the nature of representation and identity. When a physical event is reduced to mathematical description and then reconstructed from that description, what is preserved and what is lost?

The traditional answer, inherited from centuries of philosophy, is that the representation is not the thing itself. Plato's Cave allegory describes prisoners who see shadows on a wall and mistake them for reality. The shadows are representations of real objects. They share some properties with the objects but they are not the objects.

The polynomial walk is a kind of shadow. The 12 coefficients that describe the walker's trajectory are not the walker. They are numbers. They are abstract mathematical objects that, when evaluated at specific times, produce positions in space. The rendering engine uses these positions to produce images that resemble the original walk.

But there is a difference between the polynomial shadow and Plato's shadow. The polynomial shadow contains all of the information that was in the original walk, within the resolution of the capture. The man's position at every instant is reproduced exactly. The static environment is reproduced exactly. The camera perspective is reproduced exactly. The reconstruction is not a partial shadow. It is a complete reproduction of the visual content.

This raises the philosophical question in its sharpest form. If the reconstruction is visually identical to the original, in what sense is it different? If a viewer cannot distinguish the polynomial reconstruction from the original video, does the difference matter?

The answer depends on what we mean by the walk. If the walk is the physical act of a man moving through space, the polynomial description is not the walk. It is a description of the walk. The man's experience, the heat of the Pompeii sun, the texture of the ancient stones, the sound of his footsteps, these are not captured by 12 coefficients.

But if the walk is the visual information that a camera captures, the polynomial description contains all of it. Every frame of the original video is reproduced by the polynomial reconstruction. The visual content is preserved perfectly.

The 700,000 to 1 compression ratio raises this question with unusual clarity. The gap between the original and the representation is vast in terms of data. But the gap between the original and the reconstruction is zero in terms of visual content. The mathematics bridges the gap completely.

Plato would recognize the question. He might not recognize the answer.
