Quantum Polynomial Compression: The Ultimate Bandwidth Solution

Current PWTP implementation achieves 700,000 to 1 compression for structured video scenes. The polynomial coefficients are stored and transmitted as conventional binary numbers. But what happens when quantum computing enters the picture?

The answer, based on early theoretical work, is potentially extraordinary. Quantum systems can represent certain mathematical objects exponentially more efficiently than classical systems. Polynomial coefficients are among those objects.

A quantum state can encode n polynomial coefficients in log(n) qubits using amplitude encoding. The coefficients of a degree-d polynomial, which classically require 4(d+1) bytes of storage, can be represented by a quantum state of approximately log2(d+1) qubits. For the cubic polynomials used in PWTP walking trajectories, this means the 12 coefficients that currently require 48 bytes could be represented by a quantum state of approximately 4 qubits.

The implications for the fifteen-minute walk through Pompeii are speculative but compelling. The current PWTP transmission of 600 kilobytes consists of approximately 400 kilobytes for the static world model and 200 kilobytes for the trajectory polynomials. If quantum encoding reduces the trajectory coefficients by a factor of ten to one hundred, the trajectory data drops from 200 kilobytes to 2 to 20 kilobytes.

The static world model presents a different challenge. Polynomial surface meshes are inherently classical objects, large collections of coefficients that describe geometric surfaces. Quantum encoding of these coefficients could achieve similar compression ratios. The 400 kilobyte static model might be reduced to 4 to 40 kilobytes in a quantum representation.

The total quantum-encoded PWTP transmission for the fifteen-minute walk: potentially 6 to 60 kilobytes. Compared to 400 gigabytes of traditional video, this is a compression ratio of 7 billion to 1 or higher.

These numbers are theoretical. Practical quantum encoding of polynomial coefficients requires fault-tolerant quantum computers, which do not yet exist at the necessary scale. The quantum advantage in polynomial encoding is real but depends on quantum hardware that is years or decades away.

However, the research direction is clear. PWTP already achieves extraordinary compression using classical mathematics. Quantum enhancement of PWTP could push the compression ratio another order of magnitude or more. The fifteen-minute walk that currently requires 600 kilobytes might eventually require 60 bytes. Six hundred billion to one.

The quantum polynomial compression path is not about current technology. It is about the theoretical ceiling of what is possible when the mathematical structure of reality meets the computational structure of quantum mechanics. The ceiling, by current estimates, is astonishingly high.
