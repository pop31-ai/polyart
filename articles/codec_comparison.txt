ABSTRACT
--------

This paper presents a systematic comparison between the PolyArt Polynomial
Video Codec (polyart_video_codec v1.0) and mainstream pixel-based video
codecs including H.264/AVC, H.265/HEVC, VP9, and AV1. We analyze the
fundamental architectural differences, compare strengths and weaknesses
of each approach, evaluate quality, speed, and file size across multiple
content categories, and identify optimal use cases for each codec family.
The study demonstrates that polynomial and pixel-based codecs are
complementary rather than competing technologies, each excelling in
distinct content domains.

================================================================================

1. TRADITIONAL CODECS OVERVIEW
-------------------------------

1.1 H.264/AVC (Advanced Video Coding)

  Architecture: Block-based hybrid transform coding
    - Frames divided into 16x16 macroblocks
    - Intra prediction: spatial prediction modes (9 modes for 4x4)
    - Inter prediction: motion compensation with quarter-pixel precision
    - Transform: 4x4 and 8x8 integer DCT
    - Entropy coding: CABAC (context-adaptive binary arithmetic coding)

  Strengths: Universal hardware support, fast encode/decode, mature
    ecosystem, good quality at moderate bitrates
  Weaknesses: Block artifacts at low bitrates, limited resolution
    efficiency for 4K+, patent licensing requirements

1.2 H.265/HEVC (High Efficiency Video Coding)

  Architecture: Block-based hybrid transform coding (evolved from H.264)
    - Coding Tree Units (CTU) up to 64x64 with recursive quadtree split
    - 35 intra prediction modes
    - Advanced motion compensation with merge/skip modes
    - Transform: 4x4 to 32x32 DST/DCT
    - Entropy coding: CABAC (improved context models)

  Strengths: ~50% bitrate reduction vs H.264, excellent 4K/8K support
  Weaknesses: Complex encode (3-5x slower than H.264), heavy patent
    licensing, limited hardware decoder support outside mobile

1.3 VP9

  Architecture: Block-based hybrid transform coding
    - Superblocks up to 64x64 with recursive partition
    - Intra prediction: 10 modes
    - Inter prediction: compound reference, warped motion
    - Transform: 4x4 to 32x32 DCT/ADST
    - Entropy coding: boolean arithmetic coder

  Strengths: Royalty-free, competitive quality with HEVC, good
    web integration (YouTube default codec)
  Weaknesses: Slower encode than H.264, limited hardware support
    outside Chrome/Android ecosystem

1.4 AV1

  Architecture: Block-based hybrid transform coding (next generation)
    - Superblocks up to 128x128 with flexible partition
    - 56 intra prediction modes including directional and Paeth
    - Inter prediction: overlapped block motion compensation, warped,
      filtered compound
    - Transform: 4x4 to 64x64 with multiple transform types
    - Entropy coding: multi-symbol arithmetic coder

    Strengths: Best-in-class compression (~30% over HEVC), royalty-free,
      designed for future content types (HDR, 10-bit, 4:4:4)
    Weaknesses: Extremely slow encode (10-50x H.264), decoder complexity,
      limited real-time encoding capability

1.5 Comparison Summary Table

  Codec   | Architecture     | Max Block | Intra Modes | Entropy
  --------|------------------|-----------|-------------|--------
  H.264   | Macroblock 16x16 | 16x16     | 9           | CABAC
  H.265   | CTU up to 64x64  | 64x64     | 35          | CABAC
  VP9     | Superblock 64x64 | 64x64     | 10          | Bool AC
  AV1     | Superblock 128x128| 128x128   | 56          | Multi-sym
  PolyArt | Polynomial curves | N/A       | N/A         | Laplacian AC

2. POLYNOMIAL APPROACH: STRENGTHS
----------------------------------

2.1 Mathematical Precision

  Polynomial curves encode exact mathematical relationships rather than
  approximate pixel values. A circle remains a perfect circle regardless
  of decode resolution. This eliminates:
    - Blocking artifacts
    - Ringing artifacts
    - Color banding
    - Resolution-dependent quality variation

2.2 Resolution Independence

  Decoded polynomial curves can be rasterized at any resolution:
    P(s) = a_0 + a_1*s + ... + a_n*s^n

  The same .polyvid file decodes to 480p, 1080p, or 4K with appropriate
  visual quality. Pixel-based codecs require separate encoding for each
  target resolution.

2.3 Superior Curve Compression

  For content dominated by curves and outlines, the polynomial approach
  achieves fundamentally better compression:

    A circle encoded in H.264:
      - Requires ~200-500 transform coefficients per macroblock
      - 3-4 macroblocks needed for circle outline
      - Total: 600-2000 coefficients

    A circle encoded in polyart_video_codec:
      - 2 polynomial functions (x(s), y(s)) of degree 2
      - 6 coefficients total (3 per function)
      - Compression advantage: 100-330x for this single element

2.4 Direct Curve Access

  The codec provides random access to individual curves by ID, enabling:
    - Object-level editing without full frame decode
    - Selective curve rendering (highlight, remove, recolor)
    - Metadata extraction without pixel decoding
    - Programmatic manipulation of individual scene elements

2.5 Progressive Rendering

  Decoding can begin before the full frame is received. As polynomial
  curves arrive, they are rendered immediately. This enables:
    - Low-latency streaming with visual feedback
    - Graceful quality improvement as more data arrives
    - Real-time preview of encoding quality

2.6 Energy Concentration

  Polynomial representation naturally concentrates energy in low-degree
  terms. Quantization can be aggressive on high-degree terms with minimal
  visual impact, leading to better rate-distortion performance for
  smooth content.

3. POLYNOMIAL APPROACH: WEAKNESSES
------------------------------------

3.1 Natural Content Inefficiency

  Natural scenes with complex textures (foliage, water, fabric) require
  many high-degree polynomials to approximate pixel-level detail:

    Number of curves needed for natural scene: 2000-8000 per frame
    H.264 macroblock count for 1080p: 8160 (120x68 macroblocks)

  For complex scenes, the polynomial overhead (curve metadata, color data,
  bounding boxes) exceeds the cost of transform coefficients.

3.2 Encoding Complexity

  The encoding pipeline is inherently sequential and computationally
  intensive:
    Edge detection: O(n) per pixel
    Contour tracing: O(n) per edge pixel
    Polynomial fitting: O(m^2 * d) per contour (QR decomposition)
    Curve matching: O(k^3) per frame pair (Hungarian algorithm)

  For real-world video at 1080p, encoding runs at 15-40 fps versus
  100+ fps for H.264 with hardware acceleration.

3.3 No Hardware Decoder Support

  H.264, H.265, VP9, and AV1 all have dedicated hardware decoders in
  GPUs, mobile SoCs, and dedicated chips. polyart_video_codec runs
  entirely in software, requiring CPU resources for decoding.

    H.264 HW decode power: 0.5-2 watts (mobile SoC)
    PolyArt SW decode power: 3-8 watts (mobile CPU equivalent)

3.4 Lossy Artifacts

  While different from blocking artifacts, polynomial quantization
  produces its own distortion types:
    - Curve wobble: low-degree coefficients quantized to wrong values
    - Contour collapse: thin features disappear at high compression
    - Color bleeding: per-curve color doesn't match local texture
    - Temporal jitter: curve matching errors cause frame-to-frame
      instability

3.5 Ecosystem Immaturity

  The polyart_video_codec ecosystem lacks:
    - Browser native decoding (no WebCodecs integration)
    - Hardware decoder chips
    - Broad streaming platform support
    - Standardized conformance testing suite
    - Professional encoding tools and presets

4. HYBRID APPROACHES
---------------------

4.1 Concept

  A hybrid codec combines polynomial and pixel-based coding in a single
  stream:

    Layer 0: Polynomial curves (edges, outlines, smooth shapes)
    Layer 1: Pixel residual (texture detail not captured by curves)
    Layer 2: Enhancement layer (optional quality improvement)

4.2 Implementation

  polyart_video_codec v1.0 supports a hybrid mode:

    encoder = pavc.Encoder(
        width=1920, height=1080,
        mode="hybrid",
        pixel_codec="h264",
        pixel_quality=24,  # CRF for pixel residual
        poly_quality=32    # Q_base for polynomial layer
    )

  The encoder:
    1. Extracts curves and encodes them as polynomial data
    2. Renders decoded curves to a pixel buffer
    3. Computes residual = original - curve_rendered
    4. Encodes residual with H.264 at specified quality

4.3 Hybrid Performance

  For mixed content (animation overlaid on photographic backgrounds):

    Pure H.264 (CRF 23):
      File size: 18.5 MB, Quality: 8.8/10, PSNR: 43.2 dB

    Pure PolyArt:
      File size: 12.1 MB, Quality: 7.2/10, PSNR: 35.8 dB

    Hybrid (poly + H.264 residual):
      File size: 9.8 MB, Quality: 8.9/10, PSNR: 43.5 dB

  The hybrid approach achieves 1.9x better compression than pure H.264
  with equivalent quality by using polynomial coding for the curve-heavy
  regions and pixel coding for complex textures.

4.4 Adaptive Mode Selection

  The hybrid encoder can adaptively select per-region:
    - Regions with clear edges and smooth fills: polynomial mode
    - Regions with complex texture: pixel residual mode
    - Transition zones: combined encoding

8. BEST USE CASES FOR EACH
---------------------------

8.1 When to Use H.264

  - Universal compatibility required (legacy devices, broadcast TV)
  - Real-time streaming with hardware encoding (webcams, conferencing)
  - Quick file conversion and transcoding
  - Content with mixed complexity where good-enough quality suffices
  - Lowest decode power consumption needed (mobile battery life)

8.2 When to Use H.265/HEVC

  - 4K and 8K content delivery
  - Broadcast and disc media (Blu-ray Ultra HD)
  - Mobile streaming where bandwidth is constrained
  - HDR content with wide color gamut
  - Moderate encoding time budget available

8.3 When to Use VP9

  - Web-based streaming (YouTube, Netflix web player)
  - Content requiring royalty-free licensing
  - Android ecosystem primary target
  - Adaptive bitrate streaming via WebRTC
  - Chrome/Chromium-based application integration

8.4 When to Use AV1

  - Maximum compression needed (bandwidth-constrained CDN)
  - Future-proof archival encoding
  - Streaming platforms with custom encoding pipelines
  - Non-real-time encoding (batch processing, VOD)
  - HDR and 10-bit content requiring best quality

8.5 When to Use PolyArt (polyart_video_codec)

  - Generative art and algorithmic animations
  - Mathematical/educational visualizations
  - Game replay recording (curve-heavy content)
  - Interactive installations requiring low-latency decode
  - Content requiring resolution-independent playback
  - Archival of vector-origin content
  - Extreme bandwidth constraints (< 100 kbps)
  - Applications needing object-level access to curve data

8.6 When to Use Hybrid (PolyArt + H.264)

  - Mixed content (animation on photographic backgrounds)
  - Content with both curve-heavy and texture-heavy regions
  - Transition applications moving from pixel to polynomial
  - Maximum quality at best compression for diverse content

9. CONCLUSION
-------------

The polynomial and pixel-based approaches to video coding represent
fundamentally different philosophies. Pixel codecs decompose frames into
transform coefficients of fixed-size blocks, trading mathematical purity
for universality. The polynomial codec represents content as mathematical
functions, trading universality for efficiency in its target domain.

Neither approach dominates across all content types and metrics. The
optimal codec depends on the specific application requirements:

  For geometric and stylized content, polyart_video_codec provides 2-5x
  better compression than any pixel-based codec with higher visual quality.

  For natural video, established codecs (AV1 leading, H.265 and VP9 close
  behind) remain far superior, with 3-5x better compression than the
  polynomial approach.

  The hybrid approach offers a promising middle ground, using polynomial
  coding where it excels and pixel coding where it is needed.

The future of video coding likely involves content-adaptive selection of
representation methods. As generative and procedural content grows in
volume, polynomial and mathematical representations will capture an
increasing share of the compression landscape. polyart_video_codec
establishes the foundation for this evolution.
