3D Polynomial Rendering: Wireframes, Surfaces, and Scene Composition
=====================================================================

Abstract
--------
Three-dimensional rendering of polynomial objects requires systematic
approaches to wireframe generation, surface tessellation, rotation
and transformation, perspective projection, and scene composition.
This article presents a complete 3D rendering pipeline for polynomial
curves and surfaces, including rotation matrices, perspective
projection models, surface patch rendering, scene graph architecture,
and lighting models suitable for mathematical objects. The polyart3D
module implements this pipeline with emphasis on mathematical accuracy
and artistic expressiveness.

1. Introduction
---------------
Mathematical objects exist naturally in three or more dimensions.
While 2D polynomial curves are useful, many mathematical forms -
surfaces of revolution, parametric patches, implicit surfaces -
require full 3D rendering capabilities. The polyart3D module extends
the polyart framework to handle three-dimensional polynomial objects,
providing wireframe visualization, solid surface rendering, and
complete scene composition.

2. 3D Wireframe Generation
----------------------------
Wireframe rendering displays curves and surface edges as line
segments in 3D space. For polynomial objects, wireframes are
generated by sampling curves at regular parameter intervals.

2.1 Curve Sampling
A polynomial curve in 3D:

  P(t) = (x(t), y(t), z(t))

is sampled at N equally spaced parameter values:

  t_i = i / (N-1),  i = 0, 1, ..., N-1

The sampling density should satisfy the Nyquist criterion for
the curve's maximum curvature:

  N > 2 * L * kappa_max / pi

where L is the curve length and kappa_max is the maximum curvature.
The polyart3D.wireframe.curve() function automatically determines
N from curvature analysis:

  from polyart3D.wireframe import curve as wire_curve

  points = wire_curve(lambda t: (cos(t), sin(t), t), 
                      t_range=(0, 4*pi), auto_density=True)

2.2 Surface Isoparametric Lines
A parametric surface:

  S(u,v) = (x(u,v), y(u,v), z(u,v))

is rendered as a grid of u-lines and v-lines:

  u-lines: S(u_i, v) for fixed u_i, v varying
  v-lines: S(u, v_j) for fixed v_j, u varying

The wireframe density is controlled by:

  nu = max(4, int(L_u * density_factor))
  nv = max(4, int(L_v * density_factor))

where L_u and L_v are the approximate arc lengths along each
parameter direction.

2.3 Hidden Line Removal
Hidden lines are identified by comparing segment depth against
the z-buffer initialized with surface depths, marking segments
behind surfaces as hidden and rendering with reduced opacity.

3. Rotation Matrices
---------------------
3D rotations use 3x3 orthogonal matrices. The three fundamental
rotations Rx, Ry, Rz compose as R = Rz * Ry * Rx (Euler angles).
Quaternions q=(w,x,y,z) provide gimbal-lock-free representation:

  R(q) = [1-2(y^2+z^2)   2(xy-wz)     2(xz+wy) ]
         [2(xy+wz)       1-2(x^2+z^2)  2(yz-wx) ]
         [2(xz-wy)        2(yz+wx)     1-2(x^2+y^2)]

The polyart3D.transform module provides Rotation.from_euler() and
Rotation.from_quaternion() with automatic conversion between them.

4. Perspective Projection
---------------------------
The pinhole camera maps 3D to 2D: x_screen = f*X/Z, y_screen = f*Y/Z.
FOV relates to focal length: f = (W/2)/tan(FOV/2). Typical values:
45 deg (natural), 60 deg (wide), 90 deg (dramatic), 30 deg (telephoto).

5. Surface Patch Rendering
----------------------------
Polynomial surfaces are rendered by tessellating them into triangle
meshes.

5.1 Bezier Patches
A bicubic Bezier patch:

  S(u,v) = sum_{i=0}^{3} sum_{j=0}^{3} B_i(u) * B_j(v) * P_{ij}

where B_i are cubic Bernstein polynomials and P_{ij} are the 16
control points. Tessellation subdivides u and v into MxN quads,
each split into two triangles.

5.2 B-Spline Patches
B-spline surfaces offer smoother control:

  S(u,v) = sum_{i=0}^{m} sum_{j=0}^{n} N_{i,3}(u) * N_{j,3}(v) * P_{ij}

where N_{i,3} are cubic B-spline basis functions. The knot vectors
in each direction control the parameterization.

5.3 Tessellation Strategy
Adaptive tessellation refines patches where curvature exceeds a
threshold (kappa = |S_uu*S_vv - S_uv^2| / (1+S_u^2+S_v^2)^2),
starting with 4x4 coarse grids and subdividing up to 4 levels.

6. Scene Graph Composition
---------------------------
The scene graph is a hierarchical tree structure:

  SceneGraph
    |-- TransformNode (rotation, translation, scale)
    |     |-- MeshNode (surface or wireframe)
    |     |-- LightNode (point, directional, spot)
    |     |-- CameraNode
    |     |-- GroupNode
    |           |-- MeshNode
    |           |-- TransformNode
    |                 |-- MeshNode

Each node stores a local transformation matrix. The world
transform is the product of all ancestor transformations:

  M_world = M_root * M_child1 * M_child2 * ... * M_node

The scene graph provides:

  - Automatic world transform computation
  - Dirty flag propagation for efficient updates
  - Frustum culling (skip objects outside camera view)
  - LOD (Level of Detail) selection based on distance

Usage:

  from polyart3D.scene import SceneGraph, MeshNode, TransformNode

  scene = SceneGraph()
  
  patch = BezierPatch(control_points)
  mesh_node = MeshNode(patch, material=Material(color='blue'))
  
  transform = TransformNode(rotation=[30, 0, 0])
  transform.add_child(mesh_node)
  scene.root.add_child(transform)
  
  scene.render(camera=camera, output='surface.png')

7. Lighting Models
------------------
7.1 Phong: I = I_a*k_a + I_d*k_d*(L.N) + I_s*k_s*(R.V)^n
7.2 Toon: quantizes diffuse into n_levels bands
7.3 Wireframe: I_wire = I_base*(1-0.3*cos(theta_view))

Normals are computed analytically: N(u,v) = normalize(S_u x S_v).

8. Performance Optimization
-----------------------------
Key optimizations in polyart3D:

  - VBOs for GPU-accelerated rendering, frustum/occlusion culling
  - Level of detail and instancing for repeated objects
  - Parallel tessellation across threads

9. Conclusion
--------------
3D rendering of polynomial objects requires careful attention to
transformation mathematics, projection models, surface tessellation,
and scene management. The polyart3D module provides a complete
pipeline from polynomial specification to rendered image, with
optimizations for interactive performance. The mathematical nature
of polynomial objects - analytically computable normals, exact
curvature, parametric sampling - enables rendering quality that
surpasses polygon-based approaches for smooth mathematical surfaces.
