ABSTRACT
---------
This article explores the intersection of Roman architectural heritage and
computational geometry through polynomial curve representation. We examine
how the classical orders, monumental arches, and the impossible geometries
of Giovanni Battista Piranesi's "Carceri d'Invenzione" can be encoded as
sets of polynomial curves. By mapping Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian column
profiles to parametric equations, and by modeling semicircular arches and
aqueduct elevations through piecewise polynomials, we establish a formal
language for architectural rendering. The SPQR composition -- combining
temple facades, Colosseum wall segments, Pantheon dome curvature, and
imperial eagle motifs -- is assembled entirely from polynomial primitives.
We further demonstrate how Piranesi's recursive staircases and Penrose-
impossible triangles can be expressed through nested polynomial transforms.
The resulting etching-style output, characterized by thin overlapping lines
and hatched shadow fields, bridges ancient Roman construction principles
with modern computer graphics. This work contributes to the growing field
of mathematical art history and digital archaeology.

Keywords: polynomial curves, Roman architecture, SPQR, Piranesi,
          computational geometry, Carceri, digital etching

1. ROMAN ARCHITECTURAL ORDERS AS POLYNOMIAL PROFILES
====================================================

The three classical orders of Roman architecture -- Doric, Ionic, and
Corinthian -- each define a characteristic column profile that can be
decomposed into a sequence of polynomial segments. A column shaft, when
sectioned along its vertical axis, yields a silhouette composed of:

  - The base (plinth and torus molding)
  - The shaft (entasis curvature)
  - The capital (echinus, abacus, and volutes or acanthus)

For the Doric order, the shaft entasis follows a subtle convex curve
traditionally approximated by a conic section. In polynomial form, we
represent this as a quartic:

  y(x) = a*x^4 + b*x^2 + c

where x is the normalized height (0 at base, 1 at neck) and y is the
radius offset from the shaft axis. Typical Roman Doric values yield
a = -0.03, b = 0.08, c = 1.0.

The Ionic capital introduces the volute -- a spiral that Piranesi rendered
with obsessive precision. A volute can be modeled as an Archimedean spiral
mapped through a polynomial warp:

  r(theta) = k * theta + epsilon * theta^3

where k controls the spiral pitch and epsilon introduces the tightening
visible in Roman examples. The abacus above the volute is a simple
rectangular profile, expressible as degree-1 (linear) segments.

The Corinthian capital is the most ornate, featuring acanthus leaves
arranged in two tiers. Each leaf profile can be captured by a sixth-
degree polynomial that traces the serrated margin:

  L(t) = sum_{i=0}^{6} c_i * t^i,  t in [0, 1]

The coefficients c_i encode the characteristic lobed shape, with
alternating maxima and minima creating the leaf serrations.

Roman builders applied these orders with strict proportional systems.
Vitruvius documented that the Doric column diameter relates to height
at a ratio of 1:7, Ionic at 1:8, and Corinthian at 1:9. These ratios
scale the polynomial domains accordingly, preserving visual fidelity.

2. ARCHES AND AQUEDUCTS: PIECEWISE POLYNOMIAL ELEVATIONS
=========================================================

The semicircular arch is the defining structural element of Roman
architecture. Mathematically, a semicircle of radius R centered at
the origin is:

  x^2 + y^2 = R^2,  y >= 0

For computational rendering, we parameterize as:

  x(t) = R * cos(t)
  y(t) = R * sin(t),  t in [0, pi]

However, Roman arches are not pure semicircles. The voussoir (wedge-
shaped stones) introduces a stepped radial profile. Each voussoir
subtends an angle delta_t = pi / n, where n is the number of stones.
Within each voussoir, the inner and outer arcs define an annular
segment:

  R_inner <= r(t) <= R_outer,  t in [t_k, t_{k+1}]

The keystone at the apex is typically oversized, creating a visual
emphasis that polynomial smoothing can capture with a local cubic
interpolation at the crown.

The triumphal arch multiplies this unit into a composition of three
or five bays. The central bay spans wider, with a taller arch ring,
while lateral bays are reduced. We model the full elevation as a
piecewise function:

  E(x) = { arch_L(x)   if x in left_lateral
          { arch_C(x)   if x in central_bay
          { arch_R(x)   if x in right_lateral

Each sub-arch is a scaled and translated copy of the semicircular
primitive, with entablature polynomials bridging the spandrels.

The multi-tier aqueduct (e.g., Pont du Gard) stacks three or more
arcade levels. Each tier repeats the arch unit with diminishing scale:
tier k uses radius R_k = R_0 / (1 + 0.3*k) and column height
h_k = h_0 * (1 - 0.15*k). The overall silhouette forms a tapering
elevation that can be rendered as a composite polynomial surface.

The hydraulic channel atop the structure is a shallow trough,
modeled as a parabolic cross-section:

  z(y) = alpha * y^2 + beta

with alpha controlling the channel depth and beta the rim height.

3. PIRANESI'S CARCERI: IMPOSSIBLE GEOMETRY AND RECURSIVE DEPTH
================================================================

Giovanni Battista Piranesi (1720-1778) produced his "Carceri
d'Invenzione" (Imaginary Prisons) as a series of 14 etchings that
defy Euclidean spatial logic. The compositions feature:

  - Impossible staircases that ascend and descend simultaneously
  - Penrose triangle constructions embedded in architectural frames
  - Recursive vaulted corridors receding into indefinite depth
  - Stairways that loop back upon themselves

From a polynomial perspective, these impossibilities arise from
inconsistent depth mappings. A staircase is typically a linear
function of depth:

  z_step(n) = z_0 + n * delta_z

In Piranesi's rendering, the same staircase simultaneously satisfies
two depth functions that cannot coexist in Euclidean space:

  z_forward(n) = z_0 + n * delta_z
  z_return(n)  = z_1 - n * delta_z

The viewer's eye follows one function ascending, then seamlessly
transitions to the other descending, creating the paradox. We encode
this as a piecewise polynomial with a discontinuity at the visual
pivot point:

  z(n) = z_forward(n)  for n < n_pivot
  z(n) = z_return(n)   for n >= n_pivot

where the continuity constraint z_forward(n_pivot) = z_return(n_pivot)
is deliberately violated to produce the spatial ambiguity.

The Penrose triangle appears in several Carceri plates as a structural
beam. Its construction requires three bars, each appearing to meet the
next at right angles in the local frame, yet forming an impossible
closed loop globally. Each bar is a rectangular prism whose depth
mapping follows:

  d_bar_i(x, y) = d_i(x, y) + epsilon_i * sin(2*pi*x/L)

with epsilon_i chosen to create the occlusion paradox at each
junction. The recursive depth of the Carceri vaults can be modeled
through iterated polynomial maps:

  V_{k+1}(x, y) = f(V_k(x, y))

where f is a contraction mapping that scales and translates the vault
profile, and k indexes the recursion depth. After N iterations, the
superposition of all V_k creates the characteristic infinite recession.

4. THE SPQR COMPOSITION: TEMPLE, COLOSSEUM, PANTHEON, AND EAGLE
=================================================================

The SPQR (Senatus Populusque Romanus) composition assembles four
iconic elements into a unified architectural fantasy.

4.1 Temple Facade
-----------------
The Roman temple follows the prostyle plan: a raised podium, a
hexastyle (six-column) portico, and a triangular pediment. The
pediment tympanum is an isosceles triangle with base B and height
H = B * tan(30 deg), rendered as:

  pediment(x) = H * (1 - |2*x/B|),  x in [-B/2, B/2]

The frieze below carries the SPQR inscription, modeled as letter
profiles composed of short polynomial segments forming serif
characters in the Roman capitalis monumentalis style.

4.2 Colosseum Wall Section
--------------------------
The Colosseum exterior presents three superimposed arcaded stories.
Each story is an arcade of the form described in Section 2, with
the added complexity of engaged columns in alternating orders:
Doric on the ground level, Ionic on the second, Corinthian on the
third. The wall surface between arches is modeled with a subtle
curvature:

  w(s, t) = s * (1 - s) * A * sin(pi * t)

where s is the horizontal parameter across the wall panel, t is
the vertical, and A controls the entasis of the engaged column.

4.3 Pantheon Dome
-----------------
The Pantheon dome is a semicircular shell with an oculus at the
apex. The interior profile is:

  dome(theta) = R * (1 - 0.05 * cos(8*theta)),  theta in [0, pi]

where the cos(8*theta) term represents the coffered ceiling panels
(28 coffers in five rings). The oculus at theta = 0 has radius
r_0 = R * 0.19, creating the iconic opening. The dome thickness
varies as:

  thickness(theta) = t_base * (1 - 0.6 * sin(theta))

providing structural realism.

4.4 Imperial Eagle and Laurel
------------------------------
The Roman eagle (aquila) is rendered as a collection of polynomial
curves tracing wing feathers, tail fan, and outstretched talons.
Each feather is a degree-4 polynomial:

  feather_i(t) = [x_i(t), y_i(t)]

with control points arranged to create the characteristic spread.
The laurel wreath encircling the SPQR shield uses a toroidal
parameterization:

  wreath(theta) = [(R + r*cos(n*theta))*cos(theta),
                   (R + r*cos(n*theta))*sin(theta)]

where R is the wreath radius, r is the leaf radius, and n = 12
gives the number of leaf clusters.

5. COMPUTER GENERATION: ETCHING STYLE WITH THIN LINES
======================================================

The final rendering adopts the visual language of Piranesi's etchings,
characterized by:

  - Uniform thin lines (0.3-0.5 pixel width before anti-aliasing)
  - Cross-hatching for shadow fields
  - Parallel hatching for mid-tones
  - Stipple dots for atmospheric depth

Each polynomial curve is sampled at sufficient density to produce
smooth anti-aliased lines. The sampling interval delta_t is chosen
such that the maximum chord deviation does not exceed 0.5 pixels:

  delta_t < 0.5 / max |d^2 P / dt^2|

For cross-hatching, a regular grid of line segments is intersected
with the shadow regions defined by the architectural forms. The
hatching angle typically ranges from 45 to 60 degrees, with
line spacing s_h following:

  s_h = s_0 * (1 + 0.3 * depth_factor)

where depth_factor increases with distance from the viewer, creating
a sense of atmospheric perspective through line density alone.

The polynomial curves for all architectural elements -- columns,
arches, domes, stairs, and decorative motifs -- are composited in
layer order: background sky, distant aqueducts, mid-ground Colosseum
wall, foreground temple and eagle. Each layer uses a slightly
different line weight and hatching density to establish spatial
hierarchy.

The computational pipeline:

  1. Define polynomial coefficients for each element
  2. Sample curves at adaptive density
  3. Clip curves to visible regions
  4. Generate hatching patterns within closed regions
  5. Composite layers with depth-dependent line attributes
  6. Apply final contrast and tonal adjustment

The output is a high-resolution monochrome image (minimum 300 DPI)
suitable for both screen display and print reproduction, faithful
to the spirit of Piranesi's original copper-plate etchings while
constructed entirely through mathematical primitives.

CONCLUSION
==========

Roman architecture, from the proportional systems of the classical
orders to the monumental scale of the Colosseum and the geometric
perfection of the Pantheon, provides an ideal testbed for polynomial
curve representation. The formal vocabulary of polynomial segments --
linear, quadratic, cubic, and higher-degree -- maps naturally onto
the architectural vocabulary of columns, arches, vaults, and domes.

Piranesi's Carceri extend this challenge into the domain of
impossible geometry, where polynomial depth functions can encode
spatial paradoxes that Euclidean geometry cannot resolve. The
recursive and self-referential structures of the Carceri anticipate
modern concepts in fractal geometry and computational topology.

By combining these elements into the SPQR composition, we demonstrate
that mathematics serves as a bridge between the ancient builders who
raised Rome's monuments and the digital artists who re-imagine them
through computational means. The polynomial curve, in its elegant
simplicity, becomes the common thread connecting Vitruvius to
Piranesi, and Piranesi to the algorithmic present.

The etching style of the final output -- thin lines, hatched shadows,
layered composition -- is not merely an aesthetic choice but a
mathematical one: each line is a sampled polynomial, each shadow a
computed intersection, each depth cue a function evaluation. In this
sense, every digital rendering of Roman architecture is already an
exercise in applied mathematics, whether its creator recognizes it
or not.
