ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Medieval manuscript illumination represents one of the highest achievements
of decorative art, combining intricate knotwork, elaborate letterforms,
gilded surfaces, and complex geometric tracery. This article demonstrates
how these historical art forms can be precisely reconstructed and
creatively extended using polynomial curve mathematics. We present
Celtic knotwork as polynomial paths, illuminated letter construction,
gold leaf simulation, interlace patterns, Gothic tracery, and rose
window generation. All methods are implemented in the polyart.medieval
module.

1. CELTIC KNOTWORK AS POLYNOMIAL PATHS
================================================================================

1.1 The Structure of Celtic Knots

Celtic knotwork consists of interlaced bands that weave over and under
each other at crossings. The fundamental structure is a planar graph
where:

    - Vertices are crossing points (over/under alternation)
    - Edges are band segments connecting crossings
    - The entire knot is a single closed path (or a small number
      of closed paths)

1.2 Band Path Representation

Each band in a Celtic knot is a polynomial curve with thickness:

    centerline: C(t) = (x(t), y(t))  [polynomial curve, degree 3-5]
    width:      w(t) = w_base + w_var * sin(n * t)  [polynomial modulation]
    left edge:  L(t) = C(t) + w(t)/2 * N(t)  [N = outward normal]
    right edge: R(t) = C(t) - w(t)/2 * N(t)

The normal vector is computed from the tangent:

    T(t) = (x'(t), y'(t))
    N(t) = (-y'(t), x'(t)) / |T(t)|

1.3 Over-Under Crossing Rules

At each crossing, one band passes over the other. The over-band
is drawn as a continuous curve, while the under-band has a gap.

The crossing logic:

    if band_i is_over at crossing_k:
        Draw band_i continuously through crossing_k
        Draw band_j with gap at crossing_k
    else:
        Draw band_j continuously through crossing_k
        Draw band_i with gap at crossing_k

The gap is implemented by clipping the under-band's polynomial:

    clipped_band_j(t) = band_j(t) for t not in [t_gap_start, t_gap_end]

1.4 Generating Knotwork from Grids

Celtic knotwork can be generated from rectangular grids:

    1. Create M x N grid of crossing points
    2. Assign over/under at each crossing (alternating pattern)
    3. Connect crossings with polynomial curves (Bezier or cubic)
    4. Apply width and rendering

The connection curves between crossings are cubic polynomials:

    path_segment(P1, P2, tangent1, tangent2):
        x(t) = a3*t^3 + a2*t^2 + a1*t + a0
        y(t) = b3*t^3 + b2*t^2 + b1*t + b0

Coefficients determined by boundary conditions:
    C(0) = P1, C(1) = P2
    C'(0) = tangent1, C'(1) = tangent2

1.5 Knotwork Topology

The mathematical structure is described by:

    Crossing sequence: [over, under, over, under, ...]
    Path trace: following the single continuous band
    Period: number of crossings before the pattern repeats

For an m x n grid with standard alternation:

    total_crossings = m * n
    path_length = 2 * m * n  [each crossing visited twice]

2. ILLUMINATED LETTER CONSTRUCTION
================================================================================

2.1 Letter Skeleton

An illuminated letter begins with a structural skeleton:

    skeleton: polynomial curves defining the letter's basic form

For Roman alphabet letters, the skeleton consists of:
    - Straight strokes (degree 1 polynomials)
    - Curves (degree 2-3 polynomials)
    - Serifs (small degree 2-3 polynomials at stroke ends)

Example: Letter "B"
    Vertical stroke:  x = 0, y = [0, H]  (degree 1)
    Upper bowl:       x = f_upper(y), semicircular, degree 2
    Lower bowl:       x = f_lower(y), semicircular, degree 2

2.2 Decorative Extensions

The skeleton is extended with decorative polynomial curves:

    Spiral terminals:  r(theta) = a + b*theta  (Archimedean, degree 1 in theta)
    Vine scrolls:      y(x) = A*sin(k*x) + polynomial envelope
    Flower heads:      rose curves r(theta) = cos(n*theta)

The decorative elements are placed at specific attachment points on
the letter skeleton.

2.3 Filling Patterns

The body of the illuminated letter is filled with patterns:

    Diagonal hatching: parallel lines at 45 degrees
    Cross-hatching:    two sets of parallel lines at 45 and 135 degrees
    Scale pattern:     overlapping semicircles
    Spiral fill:       concentric spirals

Each fill pattern is defined by polynomial equations:

    Hatching lines:  y = x + k*d, k = 0, 1, ..., n  [degree 1]
    Scale pattern:   (x - cx)^2 + (y - cy)^2 = r^2  [degree 2]
    Spiral fill:     r = a + b*theta, theta from 0 to N*2*pi

2.4 Border and Frame

Illuminated letters are often enclosed in decorated frames:

    Rectangular frame with corner ornaments
    Arched frame (semicircular top)
    Trapezoidal frame (narrowing toward top)

The frame border follows a polynomial path:

    frame(t) = [x(t), y(t)]  where x and y are polynomials
    border_width(t) = polynomial modulation of width

    .set_fill_pattern(pattern_type, params)
    .set_frame(frame_type, decorations)
    .render(resolution, color_scheme)

3. GOLD LEAF SIMULATION
================================================================================

3.1 Physical Properties of Gold Leaf

Gold leaf has distinctive optical properties:

    Reflectance:  R(lambda) varies with wavelength
    Thickness:    0.1-0.2 micrometers (semi-transparent)
    Texture:      slightly wrinkled (micro-scale surface variation)
    Aging:        develops patina (color shift over time)

3.2 Polynomial Reflectance Model

The spectral reflectance of gold can be approximated by a polynomial:

    R(lambda) = c0 + c1*lambda + c2*lambda^2 + c3*lambda^3

Fitting to measured gold reflectance data (wavelength 380-780 nm):

    c0 = 0.412, c1 = -2.15e-3, c2 = 5.47e-6, c3 = -4.82e-9

This cubic polynomial reproduces the characteristic golden appearance.

3.3 Surface Texture

Gold leaf surface texture is generated by polynomial noise:

    surface_height(x, y) = sum( a_n * cos(k_n * x + phi_n_x)
                               * cos(l_n * y + phi_n_y), n = 1..N )

The specular highlight depends on surface normal:

    I_specular = I_light * (R(theta_incident))^n_shininess

where R(theta) is the Fresnel reflectance polynomial.

3.4 Aging and Patina

Gold leaf aging is simulated by polynomial color shift:

    patina_color(t) = gold_color * (1 - age_factor)
                    + patina_hue * age_factor

    age_factor = sigmoid((t - t_onset) / t_duration)

This creates a smooth transition from bright gold to aged patina.

3.5 Rendering Gold Leaf

The rendering pipeline for gold leaf regions:

    1. Define region boundary (polynomial closed curve)
    2. Compute surface normals from polynomial texture
    3. Evaluate spectral reflectance polynomial
    4. Apply illumination model (diffuse + specular)
    5. Add micro-scale texture variation
    6. Composite with surrounding manuscript elements

    .compute_normal(x, y) -> (nx, ny, nz)
    .render_illumination(light_direction, camera_position) -> color
    .apply_patina(age, environmental_params)

4. INTERLACE PATTERNS
================================================================================

4.1 Triquetra Interlace

The triquetra (three-lobed knot) is a fundamental Celtic motif.
Its polynomial representation:

    Each lobe is a cardioid-like curve:
        r(theta) = a * (1 + cos(theta))  for theta in [0, 2*pi/3]

    Three lobes rotated by 120 degrees:
        lobe_k: rotate(lobe_0, k * 2*pi/3)

    The interlace is achieved by alternating over/under at the six
    crossing points.

4.2 Spiral Interlace

Spiral interlace connects multiple spiral centers:

    Spiral A: r_A(theta) = a_A + b_A * theta
    Spiral B: r_B(theta) = a_B + b_B * theta

    Connection: polynomial curve from end of A to start of B
        P(t) = (1-t)^3 * P_A_end + 3*t*(1-t)^2 * tangent_A
             + 3*t^2*(1-t) * tangent_B + t^3 * P_B_start

4.3 Key Pattern Interlace

Geometric key patterns (meander patterns) are composed of
right-angle turns:

    Horizontal segment: y = constant, x varies
    Vertical segment:   x = constant, y varies
    Corner:             quarter-circle arc (degree 2 polynomial)

    corner(t) = (cx + r*cos(t), cy + r*sin(t)), t in [0, pi/2]

4.4 Zoomorphic Interlace

Animal figures intertwined in knotwork. The animal body is a
polynomial curve with variable width:

    body(t): polynomial curve defining spine
    width(t): polynomial controlling body thickness
    head(t): specialized polynomial for head shape
    limbs(t): branching polynomial curves

5. GOTHIC TRACERY
================================================================================

5.1 Geometric Tracery

Gothic tracery divides windows into geometric patterns using
polynomial curves:

    Lancet arch:  y = a * sqrt(1 - (x/w)^2)  [semicircular]
    Pointed arch: two circular arcs meeting at a point

    pointed_arch(x, a, b, w):
        if x < 0:
            y = sqrt(R_left^2 - (x + w/2)^2) + offset_left
        else:
            y = sqrt(R_right^2 - (x - w/2)^2) + offset_right

    where R_left = R_right for equilateral arches.

5.2 Tracery Patterns

Common Gothic tracery patterns:

    Plate tracery:    simple circular openings (r = constant)
    Bar tracery:      mullions dividing the window
    Rayonnant:        radiating lines from a central point
    Flamboyant:       flame-like cusped curves

    Flamboyant curve: y = a * sin(k*x) + polynomial envelope
    Cusped curve:     r(theta) = R * (1 + epsilon * cos(n*theta))

5.3 Rose Window Geometry

A rose window is a circular tracery pattern:

    Outer circle:     x^2 + y^2 = R^2
    Inner circles:    (x - cx_k)^2 + (y - cy_k)^2 = r_k^2
    Petal curves:     r(theta) = R_inner * (1 + 0.3*cos(n*theta))

    where n is the number of petals (typically 6, 8, 12, or 16).

5.4 Pointed Arch Construction

Polynomial construction of a pointed (ogive) arch:

    Left arc center:  C_L = (-w/2 + R*cos(alpha), 0)
    Right arc center: C_R = (w/2 - R*cos(alpha), 0)

    Left arc:  y_L(x) = sqrt(R^2 - (x - C_L.x)^2) + C_L.y
    Right arc: y_R(x) = sqrt(R^2 - (x - C_R.x)^2) + C_R.y

    Arch profile: y(x) = max(y_L(x), y_R(x))

5.5 Tracery Rendering

Gothic tracery is rendered as:

    1. Define outer window frame (pointed arch outline)
    2. Place mullions (vertical polynomial dividers)
    3. Add tracery elements (circles, cusps, foils)
    4. Apply lead came (thin lines between glass pieces)
    5. Color glass with polynomial color functions
    6. Add light transmission effects

6. MANUSCRIPT PAGE COMPOSITION
================================================================================

6.1 Page Layout

A medieval manuscript page follows specific proportional rules:

    Text area:  typically 2/3 of page width, golden ratio height/width
    Margins:    inner margin < outer margin, top margin < bottom margin
    Decorated borders: polynomial-framed regions

    text_width = page_width * (1 - margin_left - margin_right)
    text_height = text_width * PHI  [golden ratio]

6.2 Border Decoration

Page borders contain:

    Vine scrolls: polynomial curves with leaves and flowers
    Geometric frames: nested polynomial rectangles/circles
    inhabited borders: animals and figures along polynomial paths
    historiated initials: decorated letters at paragraph starts

6.3 Text-Music Integration

Some manuscripts include musical notation. The neumes (musical symbols)
are polynomial curves:

    punctum:    point (dot)
    virga:      vertical line (degree 1)
    clivis:     descending curve (degree 2)
    torculus:   up-down curve (degree 3)
    porrectus:  down-up curve (degree 3)

These are drawn along the text baseline using polynomial placement.

    .add_illuminated_letter(position, letter, style)
    .add_vine_scroll(border_region)

7. COLOR AND PIGMENT SIMULATION
================================================================================

7.1 Historical Pigments

Medieval pigments and their polynomial spectral models:

    Vermilion (HgS):     R(lambda) = polynomial fitting red reflectance
    Ultramarine (lapis):  R(lambda) = polynomial fitting blue reflectance
    Verdigris (copper):   R(lambda) = polynomial fitting green reflectance
    Lead white:           R(lambda) approximately constant (white)
    Burnt sienna:         R(lambda) = polynomial fitting brown reflectance

7.2 Mixing and Layering

Pigment mixing follows polynomial blending:

    R_mixed(lambda) = alpha * R_pigment1(lambda) + (1-alpha) * R_pigment2(lambda)

Layering (glazing) follows Beer-Lambert law approximation:

    R_glazed(lambda) = R_ground * R_glaze(lambda) * T_glaze(lambda)

where R_glaze and T_glaze are polynomial functions of the glaze
pigment's reflectance and transmittance.

7.3 Vellum and Parchment

The manuscript support (vellum) has a characteristic warm tone:

    R_vellum(lambda) = 0.8 + 0.15 * exp(-((lambda - 550)/100)^2)

This Gaussian (approximated by degree-4 polynomial) creates the
slightly yellowed appearance of aged parchment.

    .glaze(thickness) -> modified reflectance

================================================================================
8. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

Medieval manuscript illumination is a treasure trove of mathematical
beauty, from the topological complexity of Celtic knotwork to the
geometric precision of Gothic tracery. Polynomial curve mathematics
provides the tools to reconstruct these historical art forms with
faithful accuracy while enabling new creative possibilities.

The polyart.medieval module brings together polynomial curves, symmetry
operations, and rendering techniques to create a complete toolkit for
digital manuscript illumination. By modeling the physical properties
of gold leaf, historical pigments, and parchment, the module achieves
a level of realism that honors the craftsmanship of medieval artisans.

Future work includes complete book of hours generation, interactive
manuscript restoration tools, and machine learning approaches to
historical style preservation.
